# Design Style: Lomo Imagery

You are designing in the **Lomo Imagery** style. High contrast, washed-out colors, light leaks, visible imperfections. Deliberately rejects polished corporate aesthetics. Frames the product as exploratory, experimental, and human — not authoritative.

## Core Principles
- **Imperfection is the signal** — grain, blur, light leaks, chromatic aberration
- **Anti-corporate** — reject polish, embrace rawness and authenticity
- **Analog warmth** — everything should feel like film photography, not digital perfection
- **Exploratory tone** — the product is a journey, not a finished statement

*These are creative starting points. Derive specific values from your product's personality and the design system you brainstorm during kickoff.*

## Typography
Mix of clean sans-serif for body and slightly imperfect type for headlines. Consider monospaced for labels, handwritten-style for accents. Clean, readable body text contrasts with the raw imagery.

## Color Mood
Warm, washed-out tones — faded amber, dusty teal, warm cream. Shadows in warm brown, not black. Accents in burnt orange or faded red. Everything desaturated. Film-like color grading: lifted blacks, rolled-off highlights.

## Techniques
- Film grain overlays (tiled texture at low opacity with multiply blend mode)
- Light leak effects (diagonal gradient overlays in warm tones)
- Image treatment: reduced contrast, desaturation, slight sepia
- Film-style borders, sprocket holes, frame numbers as decoration

## Layout
Full-bleed photography with film-style borders. Asymmetric grids with images bleeding off edges. Content overlapping images with semi-transparent backgrounds. Mix of tight and generous spacing (not uniform).

## Structural Patterns (How Lomo Pages Are Built)
Lomo pages feel like flipping through a photo album or contact sheet. The structure is visual-first — images drive the layout, text is secondary. Think darkroom, not design studio.

**Patterns that WORK for this style:**
- **Contact sheet** — images arranged in a loose grid like a photographer's contact sheet. Some larger, some smaller. Film borders visible. Text as captions underneath. Click/hover to "enlarge" like selecting a frame.
- **Photo essay** — full-bleed images alternate with text blocks. Each image tells part of the story. Text sits on semi-transparent overlays or in margins. The page reads like a photo essay in a magazine.
- **Polaroid scatter** — content blocks styled as photos (white border, slight rotation, drop shadow). Scattered on a surface texture. Not a grid — positioned semi-randomly. Draggable for interactivity.
- **Film strip** — horizontal scrolling filmstrip for features or portfolio. Sprocket holes, frame numbers. Vertical scroll for the main narrative, horizontal for exploration.
- **Darkroom reveal** — content "develops" as you scroll — starting as a light-exposed negative and developing into full visibility. Red/amber color tint transitions to full color.

**BANNED for this style:**
- Clean, polished card grids
- White or gradient backgrounds (use paper/darkroom textures)
- Pixel-perfect symmetric layouts
- Stock photography without analog treatment
- Glossy UI elements (glass, chrome, reflections)
- Standard hero → features → CTA flow (this style is visual-first, not copy-first)

## Imagery
Real photography with analog filter treatment. Candid, behind-the-scenes shots preferred. Screenshots styled as Polaroids or contact sheets. No stock photography — everything should feel authentic.

## Interactions
Slight image jitter on hover (simulating hand-held camera). Filter intensification on image hover. Film-flash page transitions. Consider a viewfinder-style custom cursor.

## Tendencies to Question
If you notice yourself doing these, pause and ask if they serve THIS product:
- Using clean, polished gradients (clashes with analog feel)
- Building pixel-perfect symmetrical layouts
- Using bright, saturated colors
- Adding glossy UI elements (glass, chrome, reflections)
